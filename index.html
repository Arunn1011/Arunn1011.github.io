How Safe Is San Francisco Now?

A 20-Year Crime Retrospective

Introduction

Crime often feels like a constant. But has San Francisco actually become more or less dangerous over time? Using over two decades of crime data, we explore how patterns have shifted — both in terms of volume and geography. This story draws on a publicly available dataset from the San Francisco Police Department, covering over 20 years of reported crimes from 2003 to 2024. The dataset includes incident-level records with information on date, time, location, police district, and crime category. We focus on “focus crimes” — such as assault, burglary, robbery, larceny/theft, drug violations, and other high-impact offenses. By analyzing these incidents across time and space, we aim to understand how the city’s safety landscape has evolved — and where risk still lingers.

Figure: Yearly trends in reported focus crimes (2003–2024)

Total Reported Crime by Year

Over the past two decades, San Francisco's reported focus crimes have followed a trajectory that tells a deeper story. In 2017, these crimes peaked — recording just over 90,000 incidents. It was a year marked by intense housing pressures, growing inequality, and shifting enforcement priorities. This spike raises questions: Was it a true surge in crime or a reflection of evolving reporting practices? Regardless, 2017 remains the city's most crime-heavy year in two decades.

What followed was unexpected. Crime began to decline after 2017, and by 2020, it plunged. This sharp drop coincided with the COVID-19 pandemic — a time when life paused, public spaces emptied, and crime opportunities diminished. But was this just a blip?

Crime Trends After 2017

The data shows the dip in 2020 wasn't just temporary. While other cities saw crime rebound post-pandemic, San Francisco’s focus crime numbers remained lower. By 2024, they had dropped by over 33% from the 2017 peak.

This trend raises important questions: Has the city truly become safer? Are long-term policies, technological interventions, or community efforts paying off? Or is it simply a change in how crime is reported and classified? Though the dataset alone can’t offer definitive answers, one thing is clear: San Francisco’s crime rates are trending downward — steadily.

But if overall crime is falling, where is it still happening? To answer that, we need to look beyond timelines — and examine the map.

Figure: Spatial comparison of crime hotspots in 2017 vs. 2024

Where is Crime Concentrated in the City?

While overall crime has declined, the geography of crime tells a more nuanced story. This map compares geolocated incidents from 2017 and 2024, categorizing them into:

  • Persistent hotspots – Crime reported in both years
  • Improved areas – Crime only reported in 2017
  • Newly unsafe zones – Crime only reported in 2024

Downtown areas like the Tenderloin, SoMa, and eastern Mission remain persistent hotspots, while other neighborhoods show improvement or newly emerging concerns.

Spatial Patterns of Change

The Mission continues to struggle — appearing as a persistent hotspot. In contrast, the Richmond, Sunset, and southern residential districts show real progress, with fewer reported incidents in 2024. However, crime hasn’t disappeared — it has shifted.

New hotspots are emerging in areas like the northern waterfront, Bayview, and peripheral zones. This suggests a reshaping of risk, not its elimination. These shifts highlight the need for evolving policy responses.

Figure: Total reported focus crimes by police district

District Disparities: Mapping the Weight of Crime

Crime patterns differ dramatically across police districts. The Tenderloin and Southern districts consistently bear a disproportionate share of reported incidents, while areas like Richmond and Taraval remain relatively calm.

These disparities shape officer deployment, public perception, and policymaking. Understanding where crime concentrates is essential for distributing resources fairly and crafting responsive strategies.

Conclusion: The Shape of Safety

San Francisco's crime landscape is changing — in volume, in space, and in complexity. While the data shows a hopeful decline in focus crime over 20 years, it also reveals neighborhoods still under pressure and new ones emerging as risk zones.

The city's safety story is not just about statistics — it's about geography. And understanding those geographies is key to building a safer, more equitable San Francisco.
